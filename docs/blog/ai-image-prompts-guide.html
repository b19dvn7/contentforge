The Art of AI Image Prompts: A Practical Guide

Updated May 2026 · 9 min read

AI image generators have exploded in capability. Midjourney V7, DALL-E 3, and Stable Diffusion 3.5 can produce visuals that rival professional photography and illustration — if you give them the right instructions. The difference between a flat, generic output and a stunning, usable image often comes down to one thing: your prompt.

This guide covers what actually works when writing AI image prompts. You'll learn how each platform interprets language differently, which modifiers move the needle, and how to avoid the most common mistakes. By the end, you'll be able to write prompts that consistently produce the images you envision.

Why Prompt Structure Matters

Every AI image model converts your text into a latent representation and then decodes it into pixels. Vague language gives the model too much freedom, producing generic results. Specific, structured language constrains the output toward your intent.

A well-structured prompt typically includes:

  • Subject — what or who is in the image
  • Action / Pose — what the subject is doing
  • Environment — where the scene takes place
  • Lighting & Color — the mood and atmosphere
  • Style & Medium — the artistic direction
  • Composition — framing, angle, and perspective

Platform Differences: Midjourney vs DALL-E 3 vs Stable Diffusion

Each model has a distinct "personality" and responds to language differently. Using the same prompt across all three will give you very different results.

Aspect Midjourney DALL-E 3 Stable Diffusion
Language style Descriptive, poetic, comma-separated keywords Natural sentences, full paragraphs Keyword-heavy, tag-like, weighted terms
Best for Artistic, stylized, cinematic visuals Photorealistic scenes, complex compositions Fine-tuned niche styles, NSFW (local), control via LoRAs
Parameter syntax --ar 16:9 --style raw --s 250 No parameters (natural language only) negative prompt:, (term:1.3), CFG scale
Negative prompts Use --no flag Describe what you don't want in plain text Separate negative prompt field
Image prompting Image URLs + text (strong) Upload image + text (moderate) Img2Img, ControlNet (very strong)

Midjourney shines when you write atmospheric, cinematic descriptions. It handles style modifiers like photorealistic, octane render, and cinematic lighting exceptionally well. Use parameters like --ar 16:9 for widescreen or --style raw for less of Midjourney's default polish.

DALL-E 3 understands natural language better than any other model. You can write "A corgi wearing a detective hat examines a tiny chalkboard" and it will nail the composition. It's weaker at fine-grained style control but excels at following complex scene descriptions.

Stable Diffusion gives you the most control through its ecosystem of LoRAs (low-rank adaptations), embeddings, and ControlNets. Prompts tend to be shorter and more tag-like: masterpiece, best quality, 1girl, solo, (detailed face:1.2). Negative prompts are essential here to suppress bad anatomy or artifacts.

Style Modifiers That Work

Adding the right style modifier transforms a generic output into something intentional. Here are categories that consistently produce strong results:

Photography Terms hyperrealistic, editorial photography, macro shot, 85mm lens, f/1.8, depth of field, film grain, Kodak Portra 400
Art Movements impressionism, art deco, art nouveau, surrealism, synthwave, vaporwave, ukiyo-e, baroque
Render Styles octane render, Unreal Engine 5, ray tracing, volumetric lighting, 3D render, claymation, isometric
Mood & Atmosphere ethereal, melancholic, serene, ominous, vibrant, muted, desaturated, high contrast, pastel tones

Lighting & Camera Terms

Lighting is one of the most underused prompt elements. Adding a single lighting term can completely change the emotional impact of your image.

  • Golden hour — warm, directional sunlight just after sunrise or before sunset
  • Cinematic lighting — dramatic, often high-contrast lighting with strong shadows
  • Soft diffused light — even, flattering light with minimal shadows, like an overcast day
  • Rim lighting — light from behind that outlines the subject's edges
  • Neon lighting — colored artificial light, great for cyberpunk scenes
  • Crepuscular rays — visible light beams streaming through gaps (god rays)

Camera terms also matter: low angle shot makes subjects feel powerful, aerial view gives a top-down perspective, and macro photography forces extreme close-up detail.

Bad Prompt vs Good Prompt: Real Examples

Example 1: Portrait

Bad
A woman sitting in a chair
Too vague. The model doesn't know age, style, lighting, or mood. You'll get a generic stock photo look.
Good
A middle-aged woman in a tailored linen blazer sits in a rattan armchair on a sunlit balcony, soft diffused natural light, warm earthy tones, shallow depth of field, editorial photography style, 85mm f/1.8
Specifies subject, clothing, setting, lighting, color palette, and photography style. The result is specific and usable.

Example 2: Product Shot

Bad
A perfume bottle
No context, no style direction. Expect a boring 3D render with no commercial appeal.
Good
A luxury perfume bottle with amber liquid, frosted glass, gold metallic cap, floating on a reflective black surface, dramatic side lighting, dark moody studio background, shallow DOF, product photography, 8K, hyperrealistic
Describes materials, lighting setup, background, and commercial photography style. Ready for use in advertising.

Example 3: Landscape

Bad
A mountain landscape
Every mountain looks the same with this prompt. No time of day, no weather, no emotional direction.
Good
A majestic snow-capped mountain peak at sunrise, alpine meadow in the foreground with wildflowers, golden hour lighting, crepuscular rays breaking through clouds, mist rising from the valley, cinematic wide shot, 16:9, ultra-detailed, photorealistic
Establishes time of day, foreground elements, atmospheric effects, and cinematic framing. The emotional impact is clear.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Overloading the prompt. More words isn't always better. 50–80 well-chosen words beat 200 loosely related ones. Stick to what's essential.
  2. Ignoring negative prompts. On Stable Diffusion and Midjourney, negative prompts remove unwanted elements (blurry, extra limbs, watermark). Learn to use them.
  3. Not specifying aspect ratio. Midjourney defaults to 1:1. DALL-E defaults to a wider aspect. Always set --ar or describe the framing so you don't crop later.
  4. Contradictory terms. Don't mix "black and white" with "vibrant colors." The model will average them into muddy results.
  5. Expecting text generation. AI image models still struggle with rendering legible text. If you need words in the image, add them in post-processing.

Iteration is Part of the Process

Professional prompt engineers rarely nail a prompt on the first try. The standard workflow is: generate → critique → refine → generate again. Note which modifiers moved the output in the right direction and which ones confused the model. Over time, you'll build a mental library of terms that work for your specific use case.
